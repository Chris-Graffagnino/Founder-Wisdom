# Strategy & Moats

Durable competitive advantage — what it is, when it becomes available, and how founders fool themselves about having it. Sources: Hamilton Helmer (*7 Powers*), Richard Rumelt (*Good Strategy/Bad Strategy*), Peter Thiel (*Zero to One*), Bruce Greenwald (*Competition Demystified*), Clayton Christensen (*The Innovator's Dilemma*), Michael Porter via Joan Magretta.

## Core axioms

**A moat requires a benefit AND a barrier — most "advantages" have only the benefit.** (Hamilton Helmer, *7 Powers*.) Power is the potential for persistent differential returns, and it exists only when something improves your cash flow (the benefit) *and* competitors can't profitably copy or neutralize it (the barrier). Being faster, cheaper, or better-designed is a benefit without a barrier — it invites arbitrage and erodes on an 18–24 month clock. When a founder says "our advantage is X," the diagnostic question is: what stops a well-funded competitor from doing X too? If the honest answer is "nothing yet," X is a head start, not a moat.

**The seven powers, by name.** (Helmer.) Scale economies (per-unit cost falls with volume), network economies (value rises with users), counter-positioning (a model the incumbent can't copy without damaging their own business), switching costs (customers bear real cost to leave), branding (durable higher willingness-to-pay from identity/trust), cornered resource (preferential access to a coveted asset — a patent, a team, a supply), and process power (embedded organizational capability that takes years to replicate, like Toyota's production system). Naming which one you're building forces a much sharper conversation than "we'll have network effects" hand-waving.

**Different powers become available at different stages — startups get counter-positioning and cornered resource first.** (Helmer's Power Progression.) In origination (pre-takeoff), the available powers are counter-positioning and cornered resource. During takeoff (rapid growth), scale economies, network economies, and switching costs become attainable. Branding and process power come last, in stability — they take years of accumulation. The practical implication: a seed-stage founder claiming brand power or process power is almost always wrong, and a seed-stage founder who can't name a counter-position or cornered resource may not have a durable business at all. *(Stage: applies to all, but the origination powers are the seed–Series A conversation.)*

**Counter-positioning is the startup's structural weapon: adopt the model the incumbent can't.** (Helmer.) The canonical pattern is Netflix's subscription against Blockbuster's late fees — Blockbuster could see the threat clearly and still couldn't respond, because matching it meant destroying their own margin. The test for real counter-positioning: if the incumbent copied you, would it materially damage their existing business? If they could copy you painlessly and just haven't yet, you have a head start, not a counter-position. Related to the disruptive-business-model lever in `customers-market.md` (Symantec/Norton) — same mechanism, different vocabulary.

**Incumbents ignore you for rational reasons, until they can't.** (Clayton Christensen, *The Innovator's Dilemma*.) Disruption starts in segments the incumbent is *right* to ignore — too small, too low-margin, serving customers they don't want. Their best customers and best financial analysis tell them to flee upmarket, which is exactly what lets the entrant improve underneath them. For founders, two implications: entering at the low end the incumbent despises is a feature, not a compromise; and the moment the incumbent starts taking you seriously is the moment your window for building takeoff-stage powers starts closing.

**Competition is for losers — escape it, don't win it.** (Peter Thiel, *Zero to One*.) Businesses in perfectly competitive markets earn nothing durable no matter how well they execute. The goal isn't to beat rivals at a shared game; it's to build something enough better (Thiel's bar: an order of magnitude on the dimension that matters) that you have the market to yourself for a meaningful window. Founders systematically misdescribe this in both directions — monopolists claim they face fierce competition, and competitive companies claim they're unique by defining their market absurdly narrowly. Do the honest version of the analysis internally even if the pitch deck does the other one.

**Start with a small market you can dominate, then expand concentrically.** (Thiel; harmonizes with Minimum Viable Segment in `customers-market.md`.) It's easier to dominate a small market than to get 1% of a huge one, and dominance — not presence — is what generates the profits and reputation that fund expansion. Amazon started with books, Facebook with one campus, PayPal with eBay power-sellers. The failure mode is the founder who fears small markets as unambitious; the small market isn't the destination, it's the beachhead.

**Barriers to entry are the only real competitive advantage; everything else is operational efficiency.** (Bruce Greenwald, *Competition Demystified*.) Greenwald's simplification of Porter: if incumbents in a market don't hold stable market share and new entrants come and go freely, there are no barriers — and in a barrier-free market, the only strategy is to be the most efficient operator, which is a treadmill, not a moat. The useful founder exercise: look at your market's history. Have share positions been stable for years (barriers exist — figure out whose) or churning (no barriers — plan to win on execution and speed, and price your ambitions accordingly)?

**Compete to be unique, not to be the best.** (Michael Porter, via Joan Magretta.) "Best" implies everyone is running the same race, which converges on price competition and eroded margins. Strategy is choosing to run a *different* race: a distinct set of activities serving a distinct set of needs, with trade-offs you deliberately accept. If your strategy has no trade-offs — nothing you're choosing to be worse at — it isn't a strategy, it's an aspiration to be good at everything, which is the same as being differentiated at nothing.

**A real strategy is a kernel: diagnosis, guiding policy, coherent action.** (Richard Rumelt, *Good Strategy/Bad Strategy*.) Diagnosis names the critical challenge — what's actually going on, simplified to the crux. Guiding policy is the overall approach for dealing with it. Coherent actions are resource commitments that reinforce each other in service of the policy. Most startup "strategies" skip the diagnosis entirely and jump to goals. If you can't state the diagnosis in two sentences, you don't have a strategy yet — you have ambition.

**Goals are not strategy; slogans are not strategy; a target ARR is not strategy.** (Rumelt.) Bad strategy has four reliable tells: fluff (buzzwords dressed as insight), failure to face the actual problem, mistaking goals for strategy ("grow 3x" is an outcome, not a plan), and bad strategic objectives (a dozen disconnected "priorities" — which means no priorities). The one-question audit for a founder's strategy doc: does it identify an obstacle and explain *how* the chosen actions overcome it? If it only describes desired results, rewrite it.

**Concentrate force on the pivot point — strategy is as much what you don't fund.** (Rumelt; rhymes with "strategy is what you say no to" in `product.md`.) Advantage comes from applying concentrated strength against relative weakness at a decisive point, and a startup's only structural edge over an incumbent is the ability to focus everything on one point while the incumbent defends everywhere. Spreading a seed-stage team across three bets isn't diversification, it's guaranteeing sub-critical force on all of them.

**"Network effects" is the most abused phrase in startup vocabulary — check the mechanism.** (Helmer's discipline applied; extends the network-effects axiom in `customers-market.md`.) Real network economies mean each user's value increases as more users join, and the market tips toward one winner. Most claimed network effects are actually scale economies (weaker), data accumulation with sharply diminishing returns (weaker still), or just "we have a lot of users" (not a moat at all). Similarly, most claimed "brand" is awareness, not durable willingness-to-pay. Before pitching a power, be able to state its benefit and its barrier in one sentence each.

**Secrets are the seed of strategy: what valuable truth do you believe that almost no one agrees with?** (Thiel.) Every durable company was built on a contrarian truth — something true about the world that the consensus rejected, which is why the opportunity was still available. If your company's premise is something everyone already agrees with, you're in a race decided by execution speed and capital, which usually favors someone better-funded than you. Write down your secret explicitly; if you can't, that's diagnostic.

**Moats are built during takeoff, not bolted on later — but PMF comes first.** (Helmer's dynamics; sequencing per the corpus.) Power without a business is a thought experiment: you need compelling value and a real market before any of this matters, which is why the PMF material in `product.md` precedes this file in a founder's life. But the window for embedding scale economies, network economies, and switching costs is the takeoff phase itself — companies that defer "the moat question" until growth slows discover the door has closed. The right cadence: pre-PMF, know which power you *intend*; during takeoff, invest deliberately in building it, even at the cost of some near-term growth.

## Common founder mistakes

- Pitching all seven powers at once. A company that claims scale economies, network effects, switching costs, and brand simultaneously usually has none of them. Investors read the list as a tell.
- Confusing operational excellence with strategy. Shipping fast, hiring well, and executing crisply are entry stakes — they're arbitrageable, and Helmer, Porter, and Greenwald all converge on this point independently.
- Doing competitor analysis instead of barrier analysis. The question isn't "what are competitors doing?" — it's "what structural feature of this market prevents entry or imitation, and can we own it?"
- Treating the strategy doc as a pitch artifact. The pitch-deck version can be optimistic; the internal version must contain the honest diagnosis, or the company is navigating with a decorative map.
- Assuming the incumbent is asleep. They see you; they're constrained. Understanding *why* they can't respond (margin structure, channel conflict, customer commitments) tells you how long your window is — and what would have to change for it to slam shut.
