# Sales & Go-to-Market

## Core axioms

**The founder must be the first salesperson.** Hiring a VP of Sales before you've personally closed 10–20 customers is malpractice. You don't know what objections sound like, what the buyer's job is, what they actually pay for, or what your real ICP is. The gate on the handoff is not a revenue number — it's whether you can write the ICP down and whether the customers you closed resemble each other (Jeanne DeWitt Grosser, Vercel/Stripe). If they don't, you have ten closed deals and no motion, and the VP is being asked to scale something that doesn't exist yet. *(Stage: Pre-seed–Series A.)*

**Your first 3–10 users should come from people you already know.** (Michael Seibel.) Founders who email investors asking "how do I find my first user?" reveal that they don't actually understand who has the problem. The idea came from somewhere — usually from observing people in your existing network struggle with something. Start with them. The "how do I get 100 or 1,000 users" question comes later and is a different question; the first few users should be findable before you write a line of code. *(Stage: Pre-seed–Seed.)*

**If your sales cycle is 6 months, your hiring cycle for sales reps is 12 months before you see ROI.** New reps need 1–2 cycles to learn, then a cycle to produce. Founders who hire reps expecting quarter-one quota attainment are setting both sides up for failure. *(Stage: Series A+.)*

**Discounting trains customers to expect discounts.** Be very careful about the precedent your first ten deals set. The discount you give to close the early customer becomes the floor for every customer in their reference network. Better to walk from a deal than to discount 50%.

**The customer who pays the most complains the least.** Cheap customers are the most expensive to serve. They generate more support tickets, demand more customization, and churn at higher rates. The temptation to "land any customer" in the early days creates a long-term cost structure problem.

**Pricing is a product decision, not a finance decision.** Most startups underprice by 2–5x. Raise prices until customers push back, then raise once more. Pricing communicates positioning: $50/month and $5,000/month attract entirely different buyers with entirely different expectations.

**Lead with outcomes, not features.** Nobody buys software; they buy the outcome the software produces. "We help RevOps teams cut close-time from 25 days to 8" is a sale. "We have a unified data layer with real-time sync" is a tour. And the outcome that closes is usually the removal of a pain, not the arrival of an upside (Grosser) — the visionary upside pitch is a founder-to-founder sale, and the product-side statement of the same pattern is the painkiller axiom in `product.md`.

**Win/loss interviews are the highest-leverage sales investment.** Talk to every customer you closed — and every prospect you lost — for 20 minutes within 30 days of the decision. The patterns are the real ICP and the real objections. Most founders do this for the first 5 deals, then stop. Don't stop — and once the volume outruns the interviews, run the same diagnostic over your call recordings (see the AI-era axioms below).

**The first ten customers are co-developers, not customers.** They're paying you to build something that doesn't exist. Treat them accordingly — give them the founder's cell number, ship fixes within hours, and make them feel like insiders. They'll be your reference list for years. *(Stage: Pre-seed–Seed.)*

**A pilot is a "no" with extra steps.** Most pilots don't convert. The ones that do convert would have signed an annual contract directly. Default to annual contracts with a 30-day satisfaction guarantee instead — same risk to the customer, better behavior on both sides.

**Procurement is a different sale than the user.** In enterprise, the person who uses the product is not the person who signs the contract. You can win the user love-fest and still lose the deal in procurement. Build the case for the economic buyer separately and in parallel.

**Sales is a discipline, not a personality.** The myth of the "natural salesperson" hurts companies. Sales is process: pipeline hygiene, qualification frameworks, objection libraries, proper handoffs. The best reps are not the most charismatic — they're the most disciplined.

**Your top of funnel is a leading indicator of next quarter's revenue.** When pipeline is shrinking, the deals you're closing now are masking the problem. Don't celebrate this quarter's close rate if next quarter's pipeline is half the size it should be.

**Inbound is a feature; outbound is a system.** Inbound-only companies hit a ceiling defined by their content marketing. Outbound-driven companies can scale to whatever the addressable market supports. By Series A, you should have both motions running, with clear ownership and metrics for each — and you start building the second one before inbound flattens, because a repeatable sales motion takes years to stand up (Grosser). *(Stage: Series A+.)*

**Channel partners take 2 years to pay back.** Founders chase channel deals to "accelerate growth" and discover that channels need the same ramp time as direct sales reps — except you don't control the rep. Channel is a long-term distribution strategy, not a short-term revenue accelerator. *(Stage: Series A+.)*

**The Big Launch is a myth.** (Paul Graham.) A coordinated press launch does not create a growth curve; relentless founder-led user acquisition does. The most memorable Big Launches are famous flops, and the companies you'd want to be launched to no one. See `yc-canon.md` for the full treatment.

**Partnerships rarely produce growth.** (Paul Graham.) A partnership with a Big Company looks like a transformative moment, absorbs six months of distracting work, and yields almost nothing — especially when results require the partner to act. Treat any partnership pitched as a growth strategy with extreme skepticism. See `yc-canon.md` for the full treatment.

**Free trials are a feature decision, not a marketing one.** Whether your product can be tried without help dictates whether you can run a PLG motion. If activation requires a human, you're inherently a sales-led company regardless of what your marketing site says. And PLG is a starting motion with a ceiling, not a destination (Grosser) — the buyers who can serve themselves run out.

**The price tag on your website is a hiring decision.** Public pricing means SMB and self-serve; opaque "contact us" means enterprise and sales-led. Companies that try to be both end up confusing buyers in both segments.

**AARRR (Pirate Metrics) is the standard GTM funnel.** Dave McClure's framework decomposes the customer lifecycle into five stages, each with its own metric and ownership:
- **Acquisition** — How do users discover the product? (Traffic, signups, paid CAC, organic CAC.)
- **Activation** — Do they have a successful first experience? (Time-to-value, completion of an onboarding milestone.)
- **Retention** — Do they return? (D7, D30, weekly active, monthly active.)
- **Referral** — Do existing users bring new ones? (Viral coefficient, NPS-driven referrals, organic-channel mix.)
- **Revenue** — Does usage convert to dollars? (ARPU, LTV, expansion, contraction.)

The discipline is two-fold. First, **diagnose your worst stage first** — most founders pour money into Acquisition when Activation or Retention is the actual leak. Fixing a leaky bucket beats running a bigger hose. Second, **assign single-owner accountability per stage** as soon as the team is big enough; without ownership, AARRR collapses into a dashboard nobody acts on.

**Design for SLIP: Simple to install, Low initial cost, Instant ongoing value, Plays well in the ecosystem.** (Michael Skok.) Distribution is a *product property* baked in from day one, not a sales motion bolted on later. Flunk any one of the four and you'll spend two years paying salespeople to overcome friction you manufactured yourself. The acid test: how many apps did you delete in the first five minutes after downloading them? Whatever did that to you, do not do to your customer. Note "low *initial* cost" doesn't contradict "charge real money early" (`customers-market.md`) or "raise prices until pushback" — the entry point is cheap to remove adoption friction; the expansion path is where the pricing power lives.

**The gain/pain ratio has to clear ~10x to overcome inertia.** (Skok.) Customers don't switch for 2x. The gain has to beat cost, training and migration pain, the startup-risk premium, and plain inertia — *all four* — before anyone changes anything. *"The greatest reason people fail is they're not solving a valuable enough problem where the gain-pain ratio is big enough that somebody will make a change."* If the founder can't enumerate the four pains the buyer must absorb, the founder hasn't yet earned the right to sell.

**Sub-three-month time-to-value is the enterprise threshold.** (Chris Gardner.) Beyond a quarter, the buyer can't build a credible payback model and the deal dies in procurement regardless of how much the user loves it. Engineer a first measurable outcome inside one quarter even if the "real" value takes a year to materialize. The corollary for product: if onboarding requires six weeks of services work, the sales motion is constrained by the services bottleneck, not by demand.

**Ask "why wouldn't you buy this?" — it's the question customers are best at answering.** (Skok.) Most founder discovery calls are gain-focused: do you have this pain, would you use this feature, how much would you pay? The richer signal is on the adoption-pain side, which buyers can describe precisely because they live it. After they've named the pain, ask the killer follow-up: "and what are all the reasons you would *not* buy my product?" The reasons are the real roadmap.

**B2B is shoe leather; B2C is marketing — don't confuse them.** (Phil Green.) *"B2B is talking to people. B2C is when you don't talk to people."* Early-stage B2B founders who run paid acquisition before they've had 200 one-on-one conversations are skipping the only learning that matters. Nobody buys a $20K/year product from an ad for a startup they've never heard of. If your motion is B2B and your calendar isn't full of customer calls, your strategy is wrong — not your funnel. *(Stage: Pre-seed–Series A.)*

## GTM in the AI era

**When products converge, the experience of being sold to becomes the differentiator.** (Grosser.) Gmail launched in 2004 with a full year of technical differentiation sitting in one feature — a gigabyte of storage — and that kind of gap has closed, because AI puts ten credible players in front of the same buyer. What the buyer can still tell apart is the selling itself, so the motion gets designed, instrumented, and iterated like a product. The limit is convergence: where you still hold a real technical lead, sell the lead.

**Automate GTM in order of workflow legibility.** (Grosser.) Inbound qualification first, low-end outbound next, enterprise prospecting last — the order tracks how completely the work can be written down. Enterprise stays research-only, because prospecting there means triangulating several layers of an organization at once; the agent prepares, the human sells. Most "AI SDR" discourse carries no sequencing at all. Start at the legible end and let the boundary move as the playbook gets written down. *(Stage: Series A+.)*

**An agent inherits its teacher's ceiling.** (Grosser.) Vercel's lead agent was trained by shadowing their best SDR, who had two years of experience; Grosser, with twenty years, kept catching sends she would never have made. Model it on your best rep and QA it with your best judge — whoever the agent copies sets the ceiling, and whoever reviews the output is the only thing that raises it. Let headcount follow the quality bar rather than lead it: ten inbound SDRs became one *[bench 2025-26]*, and only after lead-to-opportunity conversion held flat. *(Stage: Series A+.)*

**You can't automate a playbook you never wrote.** (Grosser.) An agent runs on written artifacts — the ICP, the discovery guide, the objection library — and the version that lives in a founder's head does not deploy. So the documentation founders have always deferred becomes a precondition, and the most useful second-order effect of AI on sales is that process rigor arrives years earlier. This is "sales is a discipline, not a personality" with a deadline attached. The limit: revise the artifacts on the cadence of the motion, or the agent runs last year's ICP at machine volume.

**The machine reads your losses more honestly than your reps do.** (Grosser.) Run agents over your call recordings and email threads: an AE blamed the quarter's biggest loss on price, and the agent, having read every call and email on the account, showed they had never reached the economic buyer. Reps narrate deals through memory and incentive; the recording doesn't. The principle to push on is the general one — any diagnostic worth running after you lose should eventually run while you can still win.

**Segment on what predicts value, not on headcount.** (Grosser.) Pick the signal that actually predicts spend in your product — traffic, usage, workload, whatever you meter — and write a promotion rule into the model so an account moves when its behavior moves. OpenAI at roughly three thousand employees *[bench 2025-26]* is mid-market by headcount and enterprise by traffic; AI-native companies stay small while getting huge, and a headcount-tiered model covers that account like mid-market. *(Stage: Series A+.)*

**ERR is not ARR: AI budgets are experiments wearing ARR's clothes.** (Grosser, relaying the term.) Experimental run-rate revenue is the one-year "give it a go" contract signed against a corporate AI mandate rather than an operating budget. Qualify for it on the way in — which budget did the money come from, and who owns the workflow when the mandate expires — and forecast it as trial-quality revenue rather than base. See `finance-ops.md` for the full treatment.

## Common founder mistakes

- Hiring a VP of Sales to "figure out" sales before product-market fit. The VP can scale a working motion; they cannot invent one.
- Comp plans that pay on bookings without enforcing collection. Reps optimize for what gets paid; if you pay on bookings, you'll get bookings that don't collect.
- Letting deal size creep up without changing the sales motion. The motion that closes $20K ACVs doesn't close $200K ACVs — different process, different rep profile, different cycle.
- Marketing tactics confused for marketing strategy. SEO, paid, content, events are tactics. Strategy is who you serve and what makes them choose you.
- Believing the demo. Customers love your demo because they're polite. Love in a demo doesn't predict purchase.
