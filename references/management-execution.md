# Management & Execution

How founders become managers, and how the company becomes an execution machine. Sources: Andy Grove (*High Output Management*), Ben Horowitz (*The Hard Thing About Hard Things*), Matt Mochary (*The Great CEO Within*), Elad Gil (*High Growth Handbook*), Brian Chesky and Paul Graham for the founder-mode counter-canon, and the operator canon. Most of this material activates at 10+ employees; below that, `time-energy.md` and `hiring.md` cover more of what matters.

## Core axioms

**A manager's output is the output of their organization, not their own work.** (Andy Grove, *High Output Management*.) The founder who is still measured — by themselves — on personal production past ~10 employees is optimizing the wrong variable. Grove's formula: your output equals your org's output plus the output of neighboring orgs you influence. Every hour should be evaluated by its effect on those two quantities, which is why an hour coaching a struggling lead can be worth more than a day of your own coding, and why a founder proud of still doing IC work at 40 people may be describing a problem, not a virtue. *(Stage: Series A+.)*

**Spend your time where leverage is highest — and know that some of your activities have negative leverage.** (Grove.) High-leverage activities affect many people or affect one person for a long time: training, decision-making at genuine forks, information-gathering that changes plans, well-run planning. Negative-leverage activities also affect many people — waffling on a decision the team is blocked on, showing up depressed to the all-hands, meddling in work you've delegated. The founder time audit isn't "am I busy?"; it's "which of my recurring activities multiply the team, and which divide it?"

**Manage each person according to their task-relevant maturity, not your preferred style.** (Grove.) How much structure someone needs depends on their experience *with this specific task*, not their seniority or IQ. A brilliant new VP doing something they've never done needs close, structured engagement; the same VP running a playbook they've run twice before needs objectives and air cover. The classic founder failure is one style for everyone — micromanaging veterans into resentment or "trusting" novices into failure — and then blaming the person for the mismatch.

**The 1:1 is the employee's meeting, and it's the highest-leverage hour on your calendar.** (Grove; reinforced by Horowitz.) Grove's estimate: ninety minutes of 1:1 can improve the quality of two weeks of that person's work. The employee sets the agenda; your job is to listen for what's underneath — Horowitz's test of a good 1:1 is that the employee does most of the talking, about the things that are actually bothering them. Founders who cancel 1:1s "because we talk all the time" are confusing transactional contact with the one channel where problems surface *before* they're emergencies.

**Meetings are the medium of managerial work — run them or resent them, but don't do both.** (Grove.) The fashionable founder position is that meetings are waste; Grove's position is that a meeting is where a manager does the job — supplying information, teaching, making decisions. The actionable split: process-oriented meetings (1:1s, staff meetings) should be regular, predictable machinery; mission-oriented meetings (to make a specific decision) should be rare, and each needs an owner who knows what decision it exists to produce. A meeting with no decision and no owner is the actual waste.

**Give ratings and hard feedback in person and in the moment; save praise for public.** (Horowitz, and general operator consensus.) Feedback delayed until the review cycle is feedback denied — the behavior has compounded for months. Horowitz's frame: feedback is a dialogue, delivered from support ("I want you to succeed, and this is in the way"), high-frequency, and calibrated to the person. The founder who avoids hard conversations to preserve harmony is choosing their own comfort over the employee's trajectory, and the team always knows.

**Write it down before you decide it.** (Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*; Amazon's practice.) Verbal debate rewards the fastest talker; written proposals reward the clearest thinker. For any significant decision, have the owner write one page — situation, proposal, alternatives considered — and have the room read it silently before discussing. This surfaces disagreement early, creates a record you can learn from later, and is the single cheapest upgrade to a leadership team's decision quality. *(Stage: becomes essential around Series A, when decisions start outliving the conversations that made them.)*

**Decide with the right speed for the decision type, and name who decides.** (Mochary; pairs with Bezos's Type 1/Type 2 in `product.md`.) Most execution drag isn't bad decisions — it's undecided decisions. For each open question, name a single decision-maker, a date, and whether it's one-way or two-way. Mochary's discipline: two-way-door decisions get decided by the closest competent person immediately; only one-way doors earn the full deliberation apparatus. A leadership team that leaves meetings without written decisions and owners will re-litigate the same topics weekly.

**Hire executives for the next 12–18 months, not for the five-year dream.** (Elad Gil, *High Growth Handbook*.) The VP who can take you from 20 to 100 people is often not the one who runs a 1,000-person org, and hiring the big-company executive "we'll grow into" gets you someone who can't operate without the infrastructure they had before. Hire for the stage you're entering; accept that some executive roles get re-hired as the company changes scale. This pairs with "the first executive hire is a 50/50 bet" in `hiring.md` — part of the miss rate is stage mismatch, which is at least partially controllable. *(Stage: Series A+.)*

**Give away your Legos.** (Molly Graham.) Scaling means repeatedly handing your favorite work to new people — and the anxiety that follows is not a signal something's wrong; it's what growth feels like from the inside. The founder (or early employee) who hoards responsibilities becomes the bottleneck the org silently routes around. The reframe that helps: your job isn't the Lego tower you built, it's the growing pile of new Legos — and someone taking your old job means the company is working. The one Lego this doesn't cover is the function the founder is singularly best at — see "The founder-mode counter-canon" below for that case. *(Stage: Series A+.)*

**Delegation is not abdication: match check-in frequency to task-relevant maturity, and always keep the monitoring.** (Grove.) The two failure modes are mirror images — dumping a project on someone with no follow-up ("I empowered them"), and delegating the work while hovering over every step. Grove's resolution: delegation without a monitoring system is abdication; the monitoring cadence, not the delegation itself, is the variable you tune. Handing off something you know well beats handing off something you don't, because you can actually evaluate the output.

**The leader's discipline is watching a decision you would have made differently — and letting it stand.** (Elizabeth Stone, Netflix CPTO.) Grove tells you to keep the monitoring; he doesn't tell you what to do in the moment the monitoring surfaces a call you'd have made the other way, which is exactly where delegation dies. The test at that moment is materiality rather than correctness — would this burn the place down? — and if it wouldn't, let it run and harvest the reflection afterward with the person. Override a survivable decision and you've taught the owner it was never theirs, which means you have quietly reacquired that decision and every one that resembles it. The scope is the one Grove assumes: a function you hired someone to know better than you do — where the founder is still the best-informed person on the call, the founder-mode counter-canon below governs instead.

**The CEO's calendar is the real strategy document.** (Mochary; Gil.) Whatever the strategy doc says, the company optimizes for what the CEO spends time on and asks about. Mochary's practice: block time weekly for your single top goal before anything else lands on the calendar, and audit trailing weeks against stated priorities. If your stated priority is enterprise sales and your calendar is 80% product reviews, the company has correctly learned that product reviews are the priority.

**Train your people yourself — it's the highest-leverage thing you know how to do.** (Grove.) Outsourcing all training to courses and onboarding decks skips the part only you can provide: how *this company* does the work, what good looks like here, the judgment calls behind the process. Grove, as Intel's CEO, personally taught internal courses and considered it among his highest-leverage hours. For a founder, teaching the sales pitch, the product philosophy, or the writing standard to each new cohort compounds across everyone those people later train.

**Manage the org design for the problems of the next six months, and expect to redraw it.** (Gil.) Founders treat reorgs as admissions of failure; at high growth they're maintenance. The org chart that worked at 30 people fails at 80 — spans of control break, communication paths saturate, and functions need to split. Gil's counsel: do reorgs quickly and completely once decided (a slow, leaky reorg is maximally destabilizing), communicate the reasoning, and don't design the org around any one person's feelings, including a co-founder's. *(Stage: Series B+.)*

**A leaner org needs the management layer later and the written systems earlier.** The Grove and Gil playbooks are calibrated to coordination cost — the tax that appears when communication paths saturate and spans of control break. A company doing the same revenue with a third of the headcount pays much less of that tax, so the honest advice is to add managers later than the classic thresholds suggest. But the failure mode doesn't disappear, it relocates: at low headcount every function is one person deep, so the binding constraint becomes key-person concentration rather than coordination. What that changes operationally is the sequence — defer the org chart, but pull *forward* the written systems that usually arrive with it: documented decisions, and explicit ownership recorded rather than implied. Leverage per person is higher, which also means the variance of a single departure or a single bad hire is higher. See `hiring.md` for the key-person disciplines themselves (runbooks, named seconds, cross-exposure) and the hiring bar at small headcount.

**Momentum is an execution artifact: ship on a cadence the team can feel.** (Operator consensus; pairs with the momentum axioms in `yc-canon.md`.) Teams calibrate their sense of "how we're doing" from tangible shipping rhythm more than from dashboards. Long silent stretches between releases read internally as drift even when work is happening. The weekly or biweekly demo — everyone shows what moved — is cheap machinery that keeps execution honest and makes stalls visible in days instead of quarters.

## The founder-mode counter-canon

The axioms above are the professional-manager canon — Grove, Horowitz, Mochary, Gil — and for most managers in most companies they are the right default. This section is the counter-canon: the operating model Brian Chesky rebuilt Airbnb around, with Jobs-era Apple as the named template, a lineage that reached Airbnb through Hiroki Asai, ex-Apple, who joined the company. Paul Graham labeled the pattern "founder mode" in a 2024 essay; the practice and the axioms below predate the label.

**"Hire good people and give them room" is advice written for managers, and it fails systematically when a founder follows it.** (Paul Graham, "Founder Mode.") The management canon was written by and for professional managers running companies they did not found, so a scaling founder gets advice calibrated for someone with less context and less permission than they actually have. Founder after founder reports the same sequence: delegate on that advice, end up staffed with "professional fakers" — C-level executives whose real skill is managing up — while advisors confirm the whole time that the delegation is working. The limit is real: founder mode still delegates, with autonomy boundaries that widen as trust is earned, and Graham expects the term to be abused as cover for refusing delegation the company needs.

**Never negotiate your operating model with your employees.** (Brian Chesky, Lenny's Podcast, 2023.) The founder who takes the pushback personally apologizes their way to a midpoint between how they want to run the company and what the team says it prefers, and the midpoint is the one setting that makes everyone unhappy — what teams want is not their preferred process but clarity about which process they are in. The compromise fails on its own terms too: the appeased often leave regardless, and Airbnb kept running projects whose only internal advocate was long gone. Feedback on the operating model is worth hearing; it is not a vote.

**Being in the details is not micromanagement.** (Chesky.) Micromanagement is telling people what to do; being in the details is knowing what the work is — the two get conflated because from a distance they look identical, which is how a founder gets talked out of knowing their own company. Chesky's test is the board: a board cannot evaluate a CEO without the details either, and nobody calls a well-informed board micromanagers. The cost is real and front-loaded — roughly two years — and then it gives the calendar back, because Chesky's surprises flipped from nine bad out of ten to nine good out of ten, and only bad surprises consume a CEO's time.

**The less involved the founder gets, the slower the team moves — and everyone blames the involvement.** (Chesky.) The loop is self-reinforcing, which is what makes it a trap rather than a mistake: the founder steps back to empower the team, decisions stall because the person with the most context is no longer in the room, the symptom reads as "we still aren't empowered enough," and the founder pulls back further. Every turn is locally reasonable, and the company ends up unable to move at founder speed while everyone congratulates themselves on the delegation.

**Don't delegate the thing you're best at.** (Chesky, crediting Ben Horowitz.) The standard advice is to hand off what others can do so you can spend your time on what only you can do, and founders reliably invert it: the product-led founder who hires a CPO has delegated the hardest-to-replace thing they do and kept the parts anyone could have run. Airbnb's version of the discipline is structural rather than aspirational — there is no CPO title. Which function you hand off first is a different question from whether you delegate at all, and this axiom only answers the first.

**Review the assembled product on a cadence and you can feel one engineer's blockage from the CEO's chair.** (Chesky.) This is the sharpening of the demo cadence above: the demo shows what moved, the review of the *assembled* thing shows whether it cohered. The machinery at Airbnb is a calendar of CEO reviews at one, two, four, eight, or twelve weeks depending on the project, with each piece of work tracked green, yellow, or red all the way to ship. The corollary is Chesky's stated reason for never mandating a return to office: if you can review the work, you don't need to watch the workers. *(Stage: Series A+.)*

**Engage directly wherever the important work is, not only through the org chart.** (Paul Graham.) Manager mode treats the org chart as modular; founder mode treats contact two levels down as normal rather than exceptional. Jobs ran an annual retreat for the hundred most important people at Apple, explicitly not the hundred highest-ranked — the mechanism is unmediated contact with ground truth, which is what the "professional fakers" filter out. The pattern shows up independently: Jensen Huang runs NVIDIA with roughly sixty direct reports, no 1:1s, and his reasoning worked through in public in front of the whole team rather than privately. Graham flags founder mode as hypothesis-stage rather than a proven playbook, and non-founder CEOs imitating it produce a mess.

**Bureaucracy is a causal cascade that starts with dependencies, not a failure of culture.** (Chesky.) The chain is mechanical: autonomous teams each pick their own stack, divergent stacks produce a platform backlog, the backlog produces pressure to let teams self-provision, self-provisioning hardens into divisions, divisions have to advocate to get anything from each other, advocacy is politics, politics produces meetings about meetings, and the terminal state is complacency. Every step is a locally sensible response to the one before it, which is why exhorting people to be less political never works — you are asking them to stop responding rationally to the structure you built. The intervention is upstream and structural: remove dependencies, remove divisions.

**Metrics are subordinate to the calendar.** (Chesky.) Ship continuously — the momentum axiom above governs that — but launch on a fixed rhythm, Airbnb's being twice a year, and let the rhythm rather than the dashboard set the company's tempo. A metric target tells one team what to optimize; a launch date tells the whole company when everything has to be true at once, which is the only forcing function that gets product, marketing, support, and ops to converge. This doesn't overturn "pick one metric and grow it" in `yc-canon.md`; the scopes differ — one metric governs whether the product works at all, the calendar governs how a company with a dozen teams converges on a coherent one. See `product.md` for the hypothesis discipline that keeps those teams from optimizing their surfaces apart. *(Stage: Post-PMF.)*

**Founder mode and the Grove canon aren't competing theories — they're indexed to how much context the person in the chair has.** Grove and Gil describe a hired manager running a function they understand better than the CEO does, where delegation with a monitoring cadence is correct and the founder who overrides it destroys the information they hired for. Chesky and Graham describe the founder, who has more context than anyone they could hire and for whom the same advice produces a company they gradually stop recognizing. That scope rule settles the collisions in this file: "give away your Legos" is about hoarding work while founder mode is about losing contact with reality — you can hand off the work and keep the contact — and Grove's warning about meddling in delegated work is written about a manager reaching into a function someone else knows better, not about a founder staying close to the one they know best. It also bounds `hiring.md`'s "the player-coach role is usually a lie": founder mode asks leaders to know the work, not to do it. The honest limit is that all of this is pattern-matched from founder-led product companies where the founder has maximal context; it is not a general theory of management.

## AI-era operating axioms

The axioms below are indexed to the current tooling moment *[bench 2025-26]* — the mechanics are durable, the market state they assume is not.

**The agent can do the work; it can never hold the accountability.** (Stone.) Accountability isn't a task that can be handed off — it's the property of a named person who has to explain the outcome, absorb the consequence, and be trusted with the next one, and "the model produced that" names a cause while transferring nothing. The cost of getting it wrong is specific: the incident whose postmortem ends with no owner is the incident that ships again, because nobody was on the hook for the fix. The limit is narrow — the rule constrains who answers for the outcome, not how much of the work the tool does — but an owner who could not have inspected what was generated is holding a fiction, which is what the next axiom is about.

**Writing the code is becoming optional; understanding the system never will be.** (Stone.) Everything downstream of "ship fast, fix fast" assumes you can reason about what you just shipped — you cannot fail fast in a system you can't explain, because the recovery step requires knowing what depends on what. When generation gets cheap and comprehension doesn't, comprehension becomes the binding constraint, which argues for reviewing the design and the reasoning behind it rather than the volume of output; the hiring-side version is "hire systems thinkers over narrow specialists" in `hiring.md`. The same constraint shows up on the management side, where understanding is the one thing a leader cannot delegate (Chesky, crediting Charles Eames).

**AI ends the tribal-knowledge moat — encode what good looks like, or it stays trapped in the veterans' heads.** (Stone.) What made a long-tenured person irreplaceable was undocumented judgment: the standard, the failure modes, the reasons behind the process. Tools that work from what you've written can only propagate the judgment you actually wrote down, which turns documentation from a compliance chore into a leverage instrument and turns the veteran who never wrote anything down from an asset into a bottleneck. The corollary is the usable part: treat the AI wave as the forcing function to fund the internal infrastructure you already knew you needed, because the work that never cleared the bar as "documentation" clears it as the thing everything else now depends on.

## Common founder mistakes

- Refusing to become a manager. "I hate process" is a personality statement; past 15–20 people it becomes a tax every employee pays. The minimum viable management system — 1:1s, a weekly leadership meeting, written goals — is small, and its absence is much more expensive than its overhead.
- Copying big-company process wholesale. The opposite error: importing OKR ceremonies, leveling matrices, and committee structures at 25 people because an advisor's company had them at 2,500. Adopt process when a specific recurring pain demands it, not preemptively.
- Holding the staff meeting as a status readout. Status belongs in writing; the meeting is for the topics that need the room — decisions, conflicts, cross-team problems. If your staff meeting could be an email, the expensive part (your leaders, together, weekly) is being wasted.
- Keeping a struggling executive too long because "the team loves them." The team also sees the misses. Firing fast applies with more force at the executive level, where the blast radius of underperformance is the entire function — and the exec exit is read by the org as a verdict on the function's strategy, which changes how you communicate it. See the "How to fire someone" section in `hiring.md` for the mechanics.
- Treating management as beneath the "real work." In a company past PMF, management *is* the real work — the product ships through the org, and the org is the founder's actual product.
