# Management & Execution

How founders become managers, and how the company becomes an execution machine. Sources: Andy Grove (*High Output Management*), Ben Horowitz (*The Hard Thing About Hard Things*), Matt Mochary (*The Great CEO Within*), Elad Gil (*High Growth Handbook*), and the operator canon. Most of this material activates at 10+ employees; below that, `time-energy.md` and `hiring.md` cover more of what matters.

## Core axioms

**A manager's output is the output of their organization, not their own work.** (Andy Grove, *High Output Management*.) The founder who is still measured — by themselves — on personal production past ~10 employees is optimizing the wrong variable. Grove's formula: your output equals your org's output plus the output of neighboring orgs you influence. Every hour should be evaluated by its effect on those two quantities, which is why an hour coaching a struggling lead can be worth more than a day of your own coding, and why a founder proud of still doing IC work at 40 people may be describing a problem, not a virtue.

**Spend your time where leverage is highest — and know that some of your activities have negative leverage.** (Grove.) High-leverage activities affect many people or affect one person for a long time: training, decision-making at genuine forks, information-gathering that changes plans, well-run planning. Negative-leverage activities also affect many people — waffling on a decision the team is blocked on, showing up depressed to the all-hands, meddling in work you've delegated. The founder time audit isn't "am I busy?"; it's "which of my recurring activities multiply the team, and which divide it?"

**Manage each person according to their task-relevant maturity, not your preferred style.** (Grove.) How much structure someone needs depends on their experience *with this specific task*, not their seniority or IQ. A brilliant new VP doing something they've never done needs close, structured engagement; the same VP running a playbook they've run twice before needs objectives and air cover. The classic founder failure is one style for everyone — micromanaging veterans into resentment or "trusting" novices into failure — and then blaming the person for the mismatch.

**The 1:1 is the employee's meeting, and it's the highest-leverage hour on your calendar.** (Grove; reinforced by Horowitz.) Grove's estimate: ninety minutes of 1:1 can improve the quality of two weeks of that person's work. The employee sets the agenda; your job is to listen for what's underneath — Horowitz's test of a good 1:1 is that the employee does most of the talking, about the things that are actually bothering them. Founders who cancel 1:1s "because we talk all the time" are confusing transactional contact with the one channel where problems surface *before* they're emergencies.

**Meetings are the medium of managerial work — run them or resent them, but don't do both.** (Grove.) The fashionable founder position is that meetings are waste; Grove's position is that a meeting is where a manager does the job — supplying information, teaching, making decisions. The actionable split: process-oriented meetings (1:1s, staff meetings) should be regular, predictable machinery; mission-oriented meetings (to make a specific decision) should be rare, and each needs an owner who knows what decision it exists to produce. A meeting with no decision and no owner is the actual waste.

**Give ratings and hard feedback in person and in the moment; save praise for public.** (Horowitz, and general operator consensus.) Feedback delayed until the review cycle is feedback denied — the behavior has compounded for months. Horowitz's frame: feedback is a dialogue, delivered from support ("I want you to succeed, and this is in the way"), high-frequency, and calibrated to the person. The founder who avoids hard conversations to preserve harmony is choosing their own comfort over the employee's trajectory, and the team always knows.

**Write it down before you decide it.** (Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*; Amazon's practice.) Verbal debate rewards the fastest talker; written proposals reward the clearest thinker. For any significant decision, have the owner write one page — situation, proposal, alternatives considered — and have the room read it silently before discussing. This surfaces disagreement early, creates a record you can learn from later, and is the single cheapest upgrade to a leadership team's decision quality. *(Stage: becomes essential around Series A, when decisions start outliving the conversations that made them.)*

**Decide with the right speed for the decision type, and name who decides.** (Mochary; pairs with Bezos's Type 1/Type 2 in `product.md`.) Most execution drag isn't bad decisions — it's undecided decisions. For each open question, name a single decision-maker, a date, and whether it's one-way or two-way. Mochary's discipline: two-way-door decisions get decided by the closest competent person immediately; only one-way doors earn the full deliberation apparatus. A leadership team that leaves meetings without written decisions and owners will re-litigate the same topics weekly.

**Hire executives for the next 12–18 months, not for the five-year dream.** (Elad Gil, *High Growth Handbook*.) The VP who can take you from 20 to 100 people is often not the one who runs a 1,000-person org, and hiring the big-company executive "we'll grow into" gets you someone who can't operate without the infrastructure they had before. Hire for the stage you're entering; accept that some executive roles get re-hired as the company changes scale. This pairs with "the first executive hire is a 50/50 bet" in `hiring.md` — part of the miss rate is stage mismatch, which is at least partially controllable.

**Give away your Legos.** (Molly Graham.) Scaling means repeatedly handing your favorite work to new people — and the anxiety that follows is not a signal something's wrong; it's what growth feels like from the inside. The founder (or early employee) who hoards responsibilities becomes the bottleneck the org silently routes around. The reframe that helps: your job isn't the Lego tower you built, it's the growing pile of new Legos — and someone taking your old job means the company is working.

**Delegation is not abdication: match check-in frequency to task-relevant maturity, and always keep the monitoring.** (Grove.) The two failure modes are mirror images — dumping a project on someone with no follow-up ("I empowered them"), and delegating the work while hovering over every step. Grove's resolution: delegation without a monitoring system is abdication; the monitoring cadence, not the delegation itself, is the variable you tune. Handing off something you know well beats handing off something you don't, because you can actually evaluate the output.

**The CEO's calendar is the real strategy document.** (Mochary; Gil.) Whatever the strategy doc says, the company optimizes for what the CEO spends time on and asks about. Mochary's practice: block time weekly for your single top goal before anything else lands on the calendar, and audit trailing weeks against stated priorities. If your stated priority is enterprise sales and your calendar is 80% product reviews, the company has correctly learned that product reviews are the priority.

**Train your people yourself — it's the highest-leverage thing you know how to do.** (Grove.) Outsourcing all training to courses and onboarding decks skips the part only you can provide: how *this company* does the work, what good looks like here, the judgment calls behind the process. Grove, as Intel's CEO, personally taught internal courses and considered it among his highest-leverage hours. For a founder, teaching the sales pitch, the product philosophy, or the writing standard to each new cohort compounds across everyone those people later train.

**Manage the org design for the problems of the next six months, and expect to redraw it.** (Gil.) Founders treat reorgs as admissions of failure; at high growth they're maintenance. The org chart that worked at 30 people fails at 80 — spans of control break, communication paths saturate, and functions need to split. Gil's counsel: do reorgs quickly and completely once decided (a slow, leaky reorg is maximally destabilizing), communicate the reasoning, and don't design the org around any one person's feelings, including a co-founder's.

**Momentum is an execution artifact: ship on a cadence the team can feel.** (Operator consensus; pairs with the momentum axioms in `yc-canon.md`.) Teams calibrate their sense of "how we're doing" from tangible shipping rhythm more than from dashboards. Long silent stretches between releases read internally as drift even when work is happening. The weekly or biweekly demo — everyone shows what moved — is cheap machinery that keeps execution honest and makes stalls visible in days instead of quarters.

## Common founder mistakes

- Refusing to become a manager. "I hate process" is a personality statement; past 15–20 people it becomes a tax every employee pays. The minimum viable management system — 1:1s, a weekly leadership meeting, written goals — is small, and its absence is much more expensive than its overhead.
- Copying big-company process wholesale. The opposite error: importing OKR ceremonies, leveling matrices, and committee structures at 25 people because an advisor's company had them at 2,500. Adopt process when a specific recurring pain demands it, not preemptively.
- Holding the staff meeting as a status readout. Status belongs in writing; the meeting is for the topics that need the room — decisions, conflicts, cross-team problems. If your staff meeting could be an email, the expensive part (your leaders, together, weekly) is being wasted.
- Keeping a struggling executive too long because "the team loves them." The team also sees the misses. See `hiring.md` on firing fast — it applies with more force at the executive level, where the blast radius of underperformance is the entire function.
- Treating management as beneath the "real work." In a company past PMF, management *is* the real work — the product ships through the org, and the org is the founder's actual product.
