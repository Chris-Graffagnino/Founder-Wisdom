# Culture & Early Team DNA

Culture is one of the most ignored topics at the early stage — founders treat it as something to "figure out later" — and one of the most consequential. The behavior patterns set by the first 10–20 employees compound forever. This file draws heavily on Tim Brady's YC Startup School lecture on culture.

## Core axioms

**Culture is behavior, not posters.** It's the implicit set of expectations that tells employees how to behave when the rules haven't been explicitly written down. Mission statements and values posters are second-order; the real culture is what people do when they're not being watched. Founders who don't shape this deliberately end up with a culture by default, and the default is rarely good.

**The first 20 employees are your cultural DNA.** There's no magic in the number 20, but the people you have on board when you start scaling are the ones who hire and train the next wave. If they embody what you want, the culture compounds in your favor. If they don't, every mistake gets propagated and is almost impossible to reverse at 100+ employees. Get the first 20 right or accept the consequences.

**Be proud of the problem you're solving.** Founders who pick problems for ego (sounds cool at a dinner party) struggle to maintain energy when things get hard. The level of energy and enthusiasm you bring is the cultural thermostat for the whole company. If you don't identify with the problem, your team will feel it before you admit it. (Tim Brady tells the story of a founder whose employees came up to him and said, "It doesn't look like you're enjoying what you're doing." The company shut down soon after.)

**Long-term vision attracts the right people.** A vision describes the purpose of the work, not the work itself. The good examples are well known:
- Tesla: *"To accelerate the world's transition to sustainable energy."* (Not "build electric cars.")
- Microsoft (1980s): *"A computer on every desk in every home."* (Laughable at the time. Magnetic to the hobbyists who could build it.)
- Google: *"Organize the world's information and make it universally accessible and useful."* (Not "build a great search engine.")

Vision attracts a broader range of talent than work-description ever can.

**Externally-focused values beat internally-focused ones.** A culture organized around the customer is more resilient than a culture organized around how employees treat each other (though both can coexist). The classic illustration: Facebook's "Move fast and break things" gave engineers no guidance about which things shouldn't be broken — privacy boundaries went unchecked. Google's early "Don't be evil" gave engineers a guide rail even when working independently. Externally-focused values guide behavior in the absence of explicit instructions; internally-focused values do not.

**List your values, then model them.** A short list (under five things) discussed between co-founders before the first hire is enough. It doesn't need to be polished or published. The point is to have a filter for hiring. Real-world examples:
- Spotify: innovative, collaborative, sincere, passionate, playful
- Atlassian: open company / no bullshit; build with heart and balance; don't fuck with the customer; play as a team; be the change you seek

Whatever the list is, model the behavior. "Do as I say, not as I do" doesn't work. Early employees take their cues from the founders.

**Have the diversity conversation early.** Not just ethnic and gender diversity — diversity of opinion, working style, background. The default founder move is to hire from their existing network, which produces a homogeneous team fast. If you want a diverse company at 100 employees, you have to start the conversation at five employees. Waiting until "we have time" guarantees you won't.

**Put a real hiring process in place from the first hire.** Don't wing it. Have a process that screens for skills and values, evaluate after a few hires whether it's filtering correctly, and refine. A hiring process that's working at 10 employees is what gives you a chance of scaling well to 100.

**Culture work doesn't take that much time.** Most of what's required is a few conversations between co-founders — about the problem, the vision, the values, diversity, hiring. None of it requires a consultant or a brand exercise. But it requires deliberateness. The biggest mistake is treating culture as something to "figure out after Series A," at which point the patterns are set.

## Stages of culture work

- **Idea / Pre-PMF stage**: Co-founders discuss the values, vision, and problem alignment. The conversations themselves are the work.
- **PMF / Early scaling stage**: First 10–20 hires test whether the values translate into hiring practice. Audit and adjust.
- **Scaling stage (50+)**: Document the culture explicitly. Train managers in it. Start the company-wide rituals (all-hands, performance reviews) that propagate it.
- **Mature stage (200+)**: Culture becomes a defensive structure. New hires either fit or are filtered out by the existing employees. The founders' job shifts from defining culture to defending it.

## Common founder mistakes

- Treating culture as a luxury for after you have product-market fit. By the time you have PMF, your culture is already half-formed and harder to change than it would have been at 10 employees.
- Confusing perks with culture. Snacks, ping-pong tables, and offsites are not culture. Culture is what people do when nobody's watching.
- Picking values that are platitudes ("integrity," "excellence," "innovation"). The test of a real value is whether it would cause you to fire a high-performing employee who violated it. If not, it's a poster, not a value.
- Hiring fast from the founder's existing network without a deliberate process. Speed feels right; the homogeneity it produces is invisible until it's a problem.
- Letting culture form through accidents — who happened to be the third employee, what the first office layout was, what the founder happened to celebrate. Deliberate culture work doesn't take much time; passive culture formation produces results you didn't choose.
