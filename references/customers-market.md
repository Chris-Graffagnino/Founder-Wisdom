# Customers & Market

## Core axioms

**The market is the ceiling.** A great team in a small market beats a mediocre team in a giant market exactly zero percent of the time. The single most predictive factor in startup outcomes is whether the market is big enough — and almost no other factor matters if the market is wrong. Pick markets carefully; you'll spend a decade in the one you choose.

**A startup is a company designed to grow fast.** (Paul Graham, "Startup = Growth.") Not age, not funding, not technology — the growth design is the whole definition, and it rests on two co-equal requirements: a very large market has to want what you make, and you have to be able to reach and serve all of it. Founders verify the size and assume the reach, which is how a big market becomes a bounded business — and a bounded business run and funded on startup expectations is what that mistake costs.

**Distribution beats product.** A worse product with better distribution wins the market. Almost always. Founders who came up through engineering often underweight this — they believe a better mousetrap wins. It doesn't. The mousetrap that wins is the one the most people see, try, and adopt.

**Your competitors are not the threat you think they are.** Indifference and inertia kill more startups than competition. The customer who says "we're going to keep using spreadsheets" is the harder loss than the customer going with a competitor. The competitor at least validated the market.

**Startups die of suicide, not murder.** Almost no early-stage company is killed by a competitor. They die of founder fallout, running out of money because they gave up rather than ran out, failing to find PMF, or losing momentum after a fundraise. Spending time worrying about competitors in the first two years is almost always misallocation. The competitive dynamics that matter come later, after the company has earned the right to compete. *(Stage: Pre-seed–Series A.)*

**A crowded market is a good sign.** (Paul Graham, "How to Get Startup Ideas.") Competitors prove demand exists and that nothing on offer is good enough — this is the idea-stage half of the position the two axioms above state for after you've started. What the crowd costs you is a thesis: a specific account of what everyone in the market is overlooking, sharper than a promise to build a better one, and at its strongest the plan incumbents lacked the nerve to run themselves (Google). The one disqualifier is an incumbent whose lock-in stops users choosing you at all. The inverse is not a rule, though: a market that looks small and turns out big is a legitimate shape, while an obviously large market with nobody in it means you misread it.

**A well, not a puddle — build something a small number of people love, not something a large number of people like.** (Paul Graham, "How to Get Startup Ideas.") Depth is the requirement and narrowness is the byproduct: you optimize for intensity of demand, and a narrow audience is what that produces. The usable test names a person rather than a population — is anyone's need urgent enough today that they would adopt an unfinished product from two strangers with no track record? Microsoft's first product served a couple thousand Altair owners whose only alternative was machine language; nearly all good startup ideas have that shape, and lukewarm product-market fit is just churn waiting to happen. *(Stage: Pre-PMF.)*

**Find the hair-on-fire customer.** (Michael Seibel.) Customers in acute enough pain adopt a half-built v1 because their need overrides their preferences. If you can't find anyone whose hair is on fire, you have a market problem, not a product problem. See `yc-canon.md` for the full treatment. *(Stage: Pre-PMF.)*

**The market gives you signal; founders provide interpretation.** Data alone doesn't tell you what to do — you have to apply judgment. But founders who ignore data in favor of their conviction become the cautionary tales. The skill is reading signal carefully and acting on conviction once you've read it.

**Finding your first users is a search problem, not a persuasion problem.** (Gustaf Alströmer.) You're not trying to convince skeptics; you're looking for the rare people already inclined to try new things, or in enough pain to try anything. The shift from "be more persuasive" to "find the right people" changes how you spend every hour. See `yc-canon.md` for the full treatment. *(Stage: Pre-seed–Seed.)*

**Two types of early adopters worth finding.** (Alströmer.) Natural early adopters, who enjoy trying new products and bring them into their teams; and burning-problem people, whose pain overrides any concern about your polish, pedigree, or size. Different acquisition tactics work for each. See `yc-canon.md` for the full treatment. *(Stage: Pre-seed–Seed.)*

**Path dependency: your first users shape your product's DNA.** (Alströmer.) Early-adopter preferences become permanent product defaults — the product you ship in five years is a direct descendant of the one you build for your first customers now. Choose first users knowing you are choosing your defaults. See `yc-canon.md` for the worked example.

**Charge real money early.** (Alströmer; counter-intuitive.) Early adopters and burning-problem customers are rarely price-sensitive. The point of charging isn't revenue; it's feedback quality — paying customers tell you the truth, free users tell you what you want to hear. See `yc-canon.md` for the full treatment. *(Stage: Pre-PMF.)*

**Your early adopters cannot sell you to the mainstream — pragmatists take references only from other pragmatists.** (Geoffrey Moore, *Crossing the Chasm*.) The early-adopter axioms above end where this one starts. Visionaries buy ahead of the herd and tolerate an unfinished product; the mainstream buyer wants proven productivity and reference-checks with industry peers first — and a visionary is not one of those peers, because the indifference to what colleagues have tried is what voids the endorsement. The chain snaps where the revenue curve was supposed to compound: growth flattens, close rates collapse, and the fatal move is reading the stall as a lull and scaling sales into it. Two limits — only discontinuous innovations, the ones forcing buyers to change how they work, face a chasm; and the pattern is B2B, keyed to committee purchase risk, which is why Moore framed consumer adoption separately.

**Adjacent markets are seductive and dangerous.** The pattern "we're going to do X like we did Y" almost never works at the operational level. The buyer, the channel, the unit economics, the competition — all different. Most attempts to expand into adjacent markets dilute focus from the core without producing the expected growth.

**Total Addressable Market math is propaganda.** Every TAM analysis is a story. Investors know it; founders should too. The relevant question isn't "what's the TAM?" — it's "what's the serviceable obtainable market in the next 5 years?" That's usually 5–10% of the TAM number on the pitch deck.

**Network effects are the rarest and most valuable moat — and most companies don't have them.** "Network effects" gets thrown around to describe any product that benefits from more users. Real network effects mean a user's value goes up as more users join — and there are only a handful of true examples per decade. Most "network effects" companies are actually scale economies, which are much weaker moats.

**The market will reorganize itself around you only if you're an order-of-magnitude better, not 30% better.** Incremental improvements get incremental adoption. Order-of-magnitude improvements force buyers to switch and remake their processes. Founders chasing a "10% better" wedge often hit a ceiling defined by switching costs.

**Category creation is a 7-year bet.** Some founders try to invent a new category. This works occasionally — but it requires years of market education before customers know they need what you're selling. If your strategy is "we need to educate the market," budget 5–7 years and the corresponding capital.

**Timing is the most important factor founders underweight.** Right idea, wrong time fails. The technology has to be ready, the buyer has to be ready, the regulatory environment has to be ready. Founders who got the timing right look prescient; founders who were "5 years early" usually fail for the same reasons that were already visible at the start.

**Bottoms-up beats top-down in markets where the user has agency.** Developer tools, productivity software, design tools — these have shifted to bottoms-up adoption because the user can adopt the product before procurement gets involved. Top-down still wins in markets with regulated buyers (finance, healthcare, defense) and where users don't pick their own tools.

**You don't have a brand — your customers do.** Brand is what customers say about you when you're not in the room. Founders who think they can construct a brand through marketing miss the point: brand is built through every customer interaction, and the marketing just amplifies what's already there. Bad product + great marketing = a brand that means "disappointing."

**Customer obsession is a strategy, not a tagline.** Companies that win with customer obsession don't talk about it — they're organized around it. The CEO is in customer calls weekly. Product roadmaps are scrutinized for customer impact. Compensation is tied to customer outcomes. Talking about customer obsession without doing it is worse than not claiming it.

**Disruptive, discontinuous, defensible beats faster, better, cheaper.** (Michael Skok.) The "10x better" axiom hides which kinds of 10x matter. Skok's three tests: *disruptive* (a new business model, like Airbnb not owning the homes); *discontinuous* (something that wasn't possible before, like AWS); *defensible* (real switching costs, network effects, data flywheels, IP). Faster/better/cheaper invites the better-resourced incumbent to crush you on their own terms. Three orthogonal tests beat one continuous gradient — and a startup that hits all three is genuinely uncopyable for the window that matters.

**A disruptive business model can beat a better product.** (Skok, Symantec/Norton.) Their antivirus product was worse than McAfee and Dr. Solomon — but pivoting from boxed software to a signature-subscription model took out two competitors in a year and raised price per seat ~50%. Distribution is one lever; *monetization architecture* is another, and founders who treat the business model as fixed leave the biggest weapon holstered. The question isn't only "is the product better?" — it's "is the way we charge for it structurally better than how the incumbent charges?"

**Find a Minimum Viable Segment, not just an MVP.** (Des Traynor, Intercom; popularized by Michael Skok.) MVP asks "is the product right?" MVS asks "are these customers identical enough that one product serves all of them?" The MVS is the smallest segment where pain, budget, use case, and channel converge so tightly you can dominate it — sized by your ability to be the obvious answer, not by revenue potential. Found PMF that doesn't generalize? You found PMF for a segment; the decision is whether to expand or stay dominant in the original one — not whether you "really" have PMF. The anti-pattern MVS doesn't name is opportunistic deal-taking: during a crossing, being sales-driven is fatal (Geoffrey Moore, *Crossing the Chasm*) — every winnable deal outside the segment scatters the whole-product investment and the references, so no segment reaches the density at which buyers start referencing each other.

**The product-company gap is a separate problem from product-market fit.** (Chris Gardner, Underscore VC.) YouTube was one of the fastest-growing internet products of its decade and still couldn't bridge to a scalable business — Google did. PMF earns you a Series A; it doesn't earn you a company. The gap is bridged by go-to-market design, pricing architecture, and monetization sequencing — not more product. Founders who treat PMF as the finish line stall at $5–10M ARR and get acqui-hired instead of scaling. *(Stage: Series A+.)*

## Common founder mistakes

- Picking a market because it sounds exciting rather than because it has the actual properties (size, willingness to pay, accessible distribution) of a winnable market.
- Underestimating the importance of being in the right place at the right time. The same founders who succeed today might have failed 5 years ago or 5 years from now.
- Believing your own TAM math. Whatever the pitch deck says, run the company on the conservative, bottoms-up number — and never lose track of which one is real.
- Mistaking "the market is huge" for "we can capture it." Markets that look huge often have entrenched incumbents, regulatory complexity, or fragmented buyers that make capture much harder than the size suggests.
- Confusing competitive analysis with strategy. Knowing your competitors' moves is hygiene. Strategy is what *you* are going to do that's different.
