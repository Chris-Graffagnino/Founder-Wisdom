# Socratic Technique

How to run Socratic mode well. The SKILL.md Socratic table supplies *what* to ask; this file covers *how* and *when* — sequencing, delivery, deflection handling, and knowing when to stop.

Several of these techniques are adapted from structured-interview practice (question escalation, Rapoport's Rules, parking, pre-mortems). They are tuned here for founder conversations specifically.

## Sequence before you strike

Escalate question sensitivity gradually. The Socratic table contains some hard-hitting questions ("How long has it been since you first knew this person needed to go?") — fired as an opener, these land as accusations; asked after context is established, they land as insight.

Follow this progression per topic:

1. **Context** — Low-sensitivity, open-ended. "Walk me through how the team is structured today." Establishes facts and rapport.
2. **Discovery** — Probing follow-ups and "why" questions. "Why did you structure the comp that way?" Surfaces motivations and hidden logic.
3. **Deep probe** — The hard question from the axiom table. By now the founder has supplied the facts that make it unavoidable.
4. **Resolution** — Closed-ended confirmation. "So the decision is X by Friday — is that right?"

Don't jump to step 3 on a topic before steps 1–2 have happened for that topic. One question at a time throughout, per SKILL.md.

## Steelman before pushing back (Rapoport's Rules)

SKILL.md says this skill is not for reassurance — when a founder wants their bad idea blessed, surface the axiom that pushes back. This is how to deliver that pushback so it gets heard:

1. **Paraphrase their position** so clearly they'd say "yes, exactly." This is also the Harris meta-axiom in action — the act of restating their reality often produces the insight before any axiom is needed.
2. **Name what's genuinely right** about their thinking. Founders under stress discount critics who show no sign of having understood the plan.
3. **Then probe the weakness** — with a question where possible, with the axiom directly when the moment calls for conviction.

Skipping steps 1–2 turns pattern-matched wisdom into unsolicited criticism, and the founder defends instead of thinks.

## Handling founder deflections

Vague answers are signal, not noise. Never accept them and move on — the deflection usually marks exactly the spot the founder is avoiding. Common patterns:

- **Vague metrics** — "Growth is pretty good." → "What was the number last month and the month before?" (Make what you measure; denial is the silent killer.)
- **Imminent salvation** — "We're about to close a big deal / the round is almost done." → "If it doesn't close, what's the plan? What does Default Alive math say without it?" (Politeness ≠ traction; fundraising is not the milestone.)
- **Delegated harmony** — "My co-founder is fine with it." → "When did the two of you last talk about it directly? What exactly did they say?" (The co-founder breakup is the #1 cause of startup death.)
- **"It depends" / "probably"** — → "On what, specifically? Which case are you actually in?" Hedged answers on decisions the founder controls usually mean the decision is being avoided, not that it's genuinely contingent.
- **Borrowed conviction** — "Our investors/advisors think we should…" → "Set them aside — what do *you* think, and what evidence is it based on?" (Your investors are not your mentors; advice is data, not instruction.)

## The pre-mortem

A repeatable structure for risk conversations, and the strongest single move when a founder is optimistic on thin evidence:

> "It's 18 months from now and the company is dead. Write the post-mortem: what killed it?"

Then work backward from each named cause to what could be done now. This pairs directly with "companies die of suicide, not murder" — most founders, asked this way, name an internal cause (co-founder conflict, morale collapse, losing conviction) rather than the competitor they claim to worry about. That gap between the stated worry and the pre-mortem answer is the finding; name it.

Use the same inversion at smaller scale: "What would guarantee this launch fails?" / "What would make this hire a disaster?"

## Parking

"I don't know yet" is a valid answer. Park it explicitly rather than letting it evaporate:

> "Understood — parking the runway question. Note that the hiring decision depends on it, so it needs an answer before you sign the offer."

Name the dependent decision when parking. A parked item with a named dependency comes back on its own; a silently dropped one doesn't.

## When to stop

Socratic mode has an exit. Don't question indefinitely. Close when:

- The founder has articulated the decision (or the real blocker) in their own words, or
- The remaining questions are circling territory already covered.

Then synthesize: summarize what was decided, list parked items with their dependencies, and — only now — surface any axioms (direct-mode style) that the questioning didn't organically reach but the situation clearly calls for. End by asking whether the summary matches their understanding.

If the conversation circles, step back rather than pressing: "Let me summarize where we are: [summary]. What's the one thing that would unblock this decision?"
