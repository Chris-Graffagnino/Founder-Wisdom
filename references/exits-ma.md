# Exits & M&A

Running a sale process once you have decided to sell. `yc-canon.md` and `fundraising.md` cover the prior question — whether to take the Corp Dev meeting at all — and `capital-valuation.md` covers the liquidation waterfall that determines what the price actually pays you. This file covers the middle: the process itself.

Selling the company is the decision where founders are most outgunned. The person across the table has closed forty deals; you will close one. They have a standing team of bankers, M&A counsel, and diligence staff whose full-time job is this transaction; you have a company to run at the same time. Every structural feature of a sale process — exclusivity, diligence sequencing, earn-outs, escrow, retention pools — exists because a repeat player designed it, and each one transfers risk from the buyer to you. The axioms below are mostly about closing that asymmetry, because nothing else in a founder's career has this ratio of stakes to reps.

This file is not legal or tax advice. Hire M&A counsel who does this for a living — your corporate lawyer who did your Series A is usually not that person.

> **Benchmarks are calibration, not truth.** Figures marked *[bench 2026-07]* — escrow percentages, exclusivity periods, earn-out share of consideration, retention pool sizing — reflect market practice as of July 2026 and move with the M&A environment. The mechanic is durable; the number is not.

## Core axioms

**A process with one bidder is not a process — it's a negotiation you've already lost.** Price in M&A is set by the buyer's fear of losing the asset, not by your model of the business. A single interested acquirer has no such fear, and they know precisely how little pressure they're under. Before you engage seriously with anyone, build a list of every plausible strategic buyer and run them in parallel on the same timeline. Two real bidders is the difference between a market-clearing price and whatever the one buyer decides you'll accept. If you genuinely cannot produce a second bidder, know that going in and adjust your expectations rather than your process.

**A banker buys you a process, a second bidder, and a bad cop — not a higher number by magic.** What a good banker actually delivers: the capacity to run 30 conversations in parallel while you run the company, a rolodex that produces buyers you wouldn't have reached, a structured timeline that manufactures deadline pressure, and someone else to be unreasonable about price so you can stay collegial with your future boss. What they don't deliver is value that isn't there. The judgment call: for a sub-$50M sale to a buyer already at the table, a banker's fee often exceeds what they add, and experienced M&A counsel plus a board member who has sold a company may be enough; above roughly $100M the DIY case weakens sharply *[bench 2026-07]*. For anything competitive, or anything where you don't already know the buyer universe, run a banker.

**The best time to sell is when you don't need to, and the ability to walk away is the only leverage you have.** A founder who has decided they're selling has already lost most of the price. Buyers read desperation in runway, in how fast you respond, in whether you have a credible standalone plan. Keep the standalone plan real and funded — the budget where you don't sell — and be willing to execute it. This is also why runway and a sale process should never be on the same clock: starting a process with six months of cash means the buyer sets the price. See `finance-ops.md` on runway proximity.

**Everything you want in the deal, you get before signing exclusivity — after that you are negotiating against yourself.** The LOI is where the deal is actually priced. Once you sign, you've agreed not to talk to anyone else for the exclusivity period — typically 30–60 days, often extended *[bench 2026-07]* — your alternatives evaporate, your team knows something is happening, and every subsequent negotiation is between you and a buyer who knows you have nowhere to go. So the LOI must nail more than the headline: price *and* structure, cash versus stock, escrow and earn-out terms, the retention pool and who bears it, treatment of unvested options and accelerated vesting, and the outside date. Keep exclusivity as short as you can and resist automatic extensions — an extension is free optionality for the buyer, paid for by you.

**Diligence is a repricing mechanism, so surface your own problems first.** Every skeleton found by the buyer's diligence team costs you more than the same skeleton disclosed by you in week one — a discovered problem is evidence about your credibility, not just about the problem. Missing IP assignments, a contractor who never signed, unclear ownership of a key dataset, a customer contract with a change-of-control clause, revenue recognized aggressively: all of these get found. The founder-side discipline is a clean data room and a proactive issues list. The information asymmetry runs the other direction too — a strategic buyer's diligence is also competitive intelligence, so stage what you disclose, put customer names and source code late in the sequence behind real commitment, and never hand a competitor your pipeline in week one.

**Earn-outs are a way to agree on a price you don't agree on, and most of them don't pay out.** The buyer proposes the earn-out precisely because they don't believe your projections; the structure lets both sides sign a press release with a big number attached. Then the acquirer reorganizes your team, changes the pricing, folds your product into a bundle, and the metric you were measured on becomes unmeasurable. What makes an earn-out survivable: a metric you actually control (revenue you book, not profit the buyer allocates), a short measurement period, written operating covenants that constrain the buyer from starving the business, acceleration if you're terminated or the unit is restructured, and a plain-language example of the calculation in the definitive agreement. Where earn-outs appear at all, 10–30% of consideration is common *[bench 2026-07]*; if a large share of the headline price sits there, evaluate the deal at the guaranteed number and treat the rest as an option.

**Escrow means part of the price is a loan you make to the buyer against your own representations.** A slice of the consideration is held back at close to secure the reps and warranties — you don't get it on closing day, and you may not get all of it. The negotiation is about size, duration, whether it's the buyer's exclusive remedy, and where the caps and baskets sit. Reps-and-warranties insurance has become the standard way to shrink or eliminate escrow in mid-market and larger deals; it costs a premium and it is usually worth it. Where RWI isn't used, escrows of roughly 5–15% held for 12–18 months remain common *[bench 2026-07]*. Two things founders miss: the escrow is deducted from *everyone's* proceeds through the waterfall, and the fundamental reps (title, authority, cap table) usually survive longer and larger than the general ones.

**Retention packages come out of your consideration, not the buyer's pocket.** The buyer underwrites one all-in number for the transaction. Every dollar of stay bonus, retention equity, and new-hire grant for your team is a dollar that would otherwise have been purchase price flowing through the waterfall to shareholders — which is to say, largely to the founders and the option holders. This is not a reason to fight retention; a buyer who won't invest in keeping your team is a buyer who will destroy the thing they bought. It is a reason to negotiate the retention pool *explicitly and in the LOI*, know its size, and know that "we increased the retention pool" is a price cut delivered in flattering language. Related: your own retention terms are a second negotiation with different incentives from the shareholders' — disclose that tension to your board rather than letting them discover it.

**Deals die in the last 20%, and the founder's job is to behave as though this one will.** The signed LOI produces a feeling of completion that is entirely false. Between LOI and close sit diligence findings, a buyer's quarter that comes in soft, a champion who changes roles, a legal issue in a contract nobody read, and a board on the buyer's side that wasn't as bought in as your counterpart implied. The operational discipline: keep selling, keep hiring for the roles you'd hire for anyway, keep the number in the plan, and keep the circle of people who know as small as legally possible. The emotional discipline is harder — a sale process runs months, compresses the founder's identity into a number, and produces a euphoria-to-despair cycle roughly every ten days. Founders who mentally spend the money in month one make bad decisions in month three. Assume no deal until the wire lands.

**An acqui-hire is a hiring transaction with a press release, and it's priced per engineer.** When the business isn't being bought, the buyer is paying a recruiting cost — a per-head number for the technical team, plus enough to clear the balance sheet, usually structured heavily as retention that vests to the individuals over years. The consequences follow directly: most of the value goes to the people who get offers, not to the cap table; investors may take a small return or none, and often have to be asked to waive preferences to make the deal work; and non-technical staff frequently don't come along. Do the ugly arithmetic before you sign — what goes to the shareholders, what goes to the team as retention, and who is left out — and have the conversation with your investors early, because a preference waiver requested three days before close is a very expensive conversation.

**The headline number is not what you get paid — run the waterfall before you negotiate, not after.** Preference stack, participation, escrow holdback, earn-out probability, transaction expenses, the retention pool, and unvested equity all sit between the press release and your bank account. Two offers with the same headline can differ by an order of magnitude to the founders. Build the model in `capital-valuation.md` — where the waterfall mechanics and the founder dead zone are covered in full — for every offer, at every structure, and share the honest per-share number with your team before they hear a headline they'll do their own bad math on.

**Tell the team late, tell them completely, and tell them what happens to their equity.** Employee equity is where the trust in an exit is won or lost. The moment the deal is announced, every employee will compute what their options are worth, get it wrong, and ask a colleague. Prepare, before announcement, a per-person statement of vested shares, strike, the per-share consideration, the tax treatment they should take to their own advisor, and what happens to unvested equity at the acquirer. Founders who over-promise upside during the process and under-deliver at close lose the team during the retention period — which is precisely when the buyer is measuring whether they overpaid.

## Common founder mistakes

- Running a "process" with one buyer and calling the resulting price a market price.
- Signing an LOI with the price agreed and the structure vague, then discovering escrow, earn-out, and retention during exclusivity when there's no leverage left.
- Letting exclusivity roll — each extension is a free option for the buyer that costs you your alternatives and another month of distraction.
- Taking the team off the plan during diligence. If the deal dies, you've bought a broken quarter as well as no exit.
- Treating the earn-out as money. Evaluate at the guaranteed number.
- Not modeling the waterfall until the definitive agreement is drafted, and discovering the dead zone at the worst possible moment.
- Using the lawyer who did the Series A for an M&A deal. Different specialty, and the mistakes are permanent.
- Hiding the retention-package tension from the board. Your incentives and the common shareholders' genuinely diverge here; naming it early costs nothing and concealing it costs your reputation.
- Letting a strategic "acquirer" run full diligence with no real intent, harvesting your customer list, roadmap, and pricing for free.
